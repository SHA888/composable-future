## Results (43 papers)

### 35. Credible Futures
- **Authors:** Andrea Iacona, S. Iaquinto
- **Year:** 2021
- **Source:** Semantic Scholar
- **URL:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s11229-021-03275-5
- **Abstract:** This paper articulates in formal terms a crucial distinction concerning future contingents, the distinction between what is true about the future and what is reasonable to believe about the future. Its key idea is that the branching structures that have been used so far to model truth can be employed to define an epistemic property, credibility, which we take to be closely related to knowledge and assertibility, and which is ultimately reducible to probability. As a result, two kinds of claims about future contingents — one concerning truth, the other concerning credibility — can be smoothly handled within a single semantic framework.
- **Relevance note:** Strongest prior in Domain 5. Uses branching-time structures to define an epistemic property (credibility) reducible to probability — operating entirely within the propositional/semantic domain of future contingent statements. Futures are propositions evaluated on branching histories. This is categorically distinct from Composable Future, where futures are algebraic objects (4-tuples) that admit operators and laws. Iacona & Iaquinto formalize what it means to *believe* a future; Composable Future formalizes what it means for a future to *compose* with another. No overlap on composability, operators, or affordance structure.

## Synthesis

### What Exists

The formal treatment of futures in the literature falls into two distinct traditions. The first — dominant in philosophy of time and temporal logic — treats futures as propositions or branching histories in Ockhamist/Peircean frameworks. Iacona & Iaquinto (2021) represent the most rigorous recent work in this tradition: they define credibility as an epistemic property over branching-time structures, reducible to probability via Markov kernels. Prior et al. (1967), Belnap et al. (2001), and MacFarlane (2014) establish the structural machinery this work inherits. The second tradition — dominant in futures studies and foresight — treats futures qualitatively as scenarios, with no algebraic structure (Hines 2020, Ollenburg 2019). Blanchard (2023) is the closest to formal scenario generation, but uses agent-based simulation rather than algebraic specification.

Neither tradition treats futures as composable algebraic objects. The branching-time tradition formalizes futures as propositions with truth values; the foresight tradition treats them as narratives with plausibility weights. The algebraic structure of paradigmatic futures — as objects that can be sequentially bound, tensored, forked, and merged — has no precedent in either.

### Gap Statement

No existing formalization treats paradigmatic futures as composable algebraic objects admitting sequential, parallel, and branching operators with provable laws — the entire formal apparatus of Composable Future occupies a gap confirmed across both the temporal logic and futures studies literatures.

### Key Question Answer

**Does branching-time credibility overlap with typed possibility?**

Partial overlap, clearly bounded. Both involve a typed notion of what futures are accessible from a given state. But the overlap ends there:

- Iacona & Iaquinto: credibility is a probability measure over branching histories; the type is a proposition; the branching structure is given externally
- Composable Future: Φ(S₁) is a set of structurally accessible futures; the type is determined by the realized state S₁; the branching structure is generated by the fork operator

The key distinction: Iacona & Iaquinto ask "how probable is this future?" after the branching structure is fixed. Composable Future asks "what futures are even in the type?" before probability is assigned. These are sequential questions, not competing frameworks.
